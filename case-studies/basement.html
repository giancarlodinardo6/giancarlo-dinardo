The Situation

Starting from zero is actually a gift if you treat it like one.

Most accounts I walk into have history. Usually that means there is something to learn from and something to undo. This one had neither. The client had not run paid social before. No pixel data, no audience insights, no creative performance to reference.

The instinct when there is nothing to inherit is to launch quickly and let the data teach you. That is backwards. The faster you can describe the person you are trying to reach, the problem they are sitting on, and the specific moment they are likely to see your ad, the better your first batch of creative will be. The research phase is not a delay. It is the brief.

Basement renovation is a category where the emotional and financial stakes are both high. Homeowners have usually been thinking about it for a long time before they do anything about it. Understanding what is keeping them in place and what finally moves them is the entire creative challenge.

Core constraint

"Without data, the research is the data. Skipping it means briefing creative for an audience you have not described yet."

Basement renovation has a longer consideration window than most home improvement categories. The homeowner who calls today has usually been thinking about it for six to eighteen months. The creative that reaches them at the right moment needs to meet them where they actually are, not where you want them to be.
The Approach

Research first. Then build the map. Then brief.

Spent two weeks before any campaign went live doing the work that would determine what creative to test. Review mining, comment section analysis on competitor ads, and three buyer personas mapped across five awareness stages each. The map determined what we launched. Testing determined what we scaled.

01
Mine reviews and competitor comments for language
Pulled Google reviews for regional basement contractors and went through the comment sections on the five highest-spending competitor ads in the area. Looked for the specific language homeowners used before they had been exposed to marketing copy. The objections, the fears, the specific situations that drove the call. That language went directly into the briefs.
02
Map three personas across awareness stages
Three distinct buyer types emerged from the research: the homeowner who had been ignoring a moisture issue and was now worried about it seriously, the one planning to convert the basement into usable living space, and the one with a teenager or aging parent who needed the square footage now. Each had a different emotional entry point and a different objection to address.
03
Brief 14 concepts across the three personas
Each concept was built for a specific persona at a specific awareness stage. Not a batch of ads that could theoretically reach anyone. Concepts for the moisture-aware homeowner used very different language and visual context than concepts for the space-conversion buyer. The map made this systematic rather than intuitive.
04
Test in ABO with equal exposure, read at week four
All 14 concepts went into an ABO testing campaign with equal daily budgets. Set a minimum of four days per concept before drawing any conclusions. At week four, read the data at the set rate level alongside CPL. Promoted the three clearest performers to a CBO scaling campaign and began the second testing round.
Creative

What the research predicted and what the data confirmed.

The research had suggested moisture concern was the highest-intent persona. Homeowners already sitting on a problem they had been avoiding were more likely to call than homeowners who were in the planning phase. That assumption held. The concepts built for the moisture-aware buyer produced better set rates than the space-conversion concepts even when CPL was similar.

Winner
Moisture urgency video
Open on visible water damage and damp walls. Voiceover from a homeowner describing how long they had been putting off the call. No product push in the first ten seconds. The problem needed to be felt before the solution appeared.
Strongest set rate of the first batch. High-intent buyer identified in the research came through in the data.
Winner
Before and after transformation static
Split image: unfinished or damaged basement on the left, finished functional space on the right. Clean, no copy overload. The image did the work. Picked up the space-conversion buyer who was earlier in the process but still qualified.
Highest lead volume of the batch. Lower set rate than the moisture video but strong enough to keep running.
Learning
3-requirements qualifier video
Hook asking homeowners to confirm three things before watching: own their home, basement older than ten years, experienced recurring moisture. Filtered hard. Lower total lead volume but the leads that came through converted at a higher rate than anything else in the batch.
Highest conversion rate on the back end despite lowest lead volume. Worth scaling in tighter markets.
Learning
Financing angle
Monthly payment framing in headline and primary text. Generated leads but most of the volume was homeowners in the early research phase, not close to booking. Set rate was low. Kept as a retargeting option for warm audiences later in the funnel.
Lower set rate than the problem-aware concepts. Consistent with the pattern across this vertical.
Tested
Space-conversion lifestyle video
Home gym, playroom, teen bedroom. Aspirational framing focused on what the space becomes rather than the problem it solves. Reached a different buyer than the moisture concepts. Set rate was lower but the persona was real. Filed as a secondary creative direction once the primary persona was established.
Different persona, lower urgency. Worth revisiting with a warmer audience.
Tested
Authority and warranty static
Focused on years in business, number of projects completed, transferable warranty. Ran well in retargeting when people had already seen other creative. As a cold traffic first touch it underperformed. Too much assumed familiarity for someone seeing the brand for the first time.
Cold traffic underperformed. Held for retargeting sequences.
Results

The research paid for itself in the first month.

By the end of month two, the account had a clear creative hierarchy based on real performance data. The two-week pre-launch research phase meant the first batch of creative was built around real buyer language rather than assumptions. That showed up in how quickly the account found its footing.

What I took from this one

01
Research is not a delay. It is the brief. The two weeks spent before launch were not preparation for the real work. They were the real work. Every brief that came from them was built around something a real person had actually said in a review or a comment section. That specificity showed up in the set rate from week one.
02
Persona mapping determines creative priorities, not just creative variety. The three personas were not a way to make sure we covered everyone. They were a ranking. The moisture-aware homeowner was the highest-intent buyer and got the most creative attention in the first batch. That was a research conclusion, not a guess.
03
Comment section language is the fastest research shortcut in a new vertical. The objections homeowners wrote under competitor ads were more specific and more emotionally honest than anything I could have generated from a brief alone. "I have been putting this off for two years because I do not know who to trust" became the opening line of the winning creative. It was written by the audience, not by me.
Without the research phase
Generic first batch based on assumed pain points rather than language homeowners actually use
No persona prioritisation, so production budget split evenly across buyer types with very different intent levels
Two to three months of learning before the account found a creative direction that worked
Competitor ad angles running without knowing whether they were working for competitors or just spending
No map to guide the second testing round once the first batch data came in
With the research phase
First batch built from verbatim buyer language pulled from reviews and competitor comment sections
Three confirmed personas with distinct creative briefs and a clear hierarchy based on intent level
Account found its footing at week four with three promotable winners from the first fourteen concepts
Competitor comment audit surfaced the objections and questions that became hook material for round two
The creative map from the research phase structured every subsequent brief through the engagement
