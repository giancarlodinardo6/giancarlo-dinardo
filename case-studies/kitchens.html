The Situation

The CPL problem was a symptom. Two separate constraints were causing it.

By March, 37% of leads were coming from outside the client's serviceable area. The OOA rate had grown from 31.6% in January to 33.3% in February to 37% in March. For every three leads generated, more than one was a call the sales team could not book. At higher spend levels, the account would have become unworkable.

And the account's two best-performing videos — one a transformation timelapse with a 45.5% set rate at peak, the other an authority-led story with 7 attributed sales — were both running in a single adset with no backups. Lifecycle decay was already showing in the data. There was no depth to absorb more spend safely.

Core constraint

Two problems compounding each other: leads coming from the wrong places, and a creative structure with no depth behind it.

OOA (out-of-area) leads are submissions from homeowners outside the client's service geography. They inflate lead volume metrics, reduce set rate, and represent real spend with zero revenue potential.

The Approach

Each constraint had a specific cause. Each got a specific fix.

The improvement came from diagnosing precisely, not from adding more budget to a leaking system. Two parallel workstreams ran simultaneously rather than sequentially.

01
Fix the OOA problem at the structure level
Cleveland was generating leads from only 3-5 targeted zip codes but bleeding spend across a much wider metro radius. A geo-fence exclusion was applied. Beyond that, the campaign philosophy shifted toward location-first CBOs with city callouts in the creative rather than all-zips campaigns generating volume without geographic precision. A weekly OOA review cadence was established.
02
Launch a city CBO for the top three DMAs
A DMA-level CBO would have been the natural choice but DMA targeting prevents using the city name in the creative copy. Three cities had sufficient audience size and strong historical performance: Columbus (240k, 31% of lifetime leads), Cincinnati (300k, 14% CoM), Dayton (220k, 10.8% CoM, $169 CPL). A city CBO allowed city-specific callouts in headline and primary text.
03
Build creative depth behind the two winning ads
Two videos were already converting — one built around the transformation, one around the company's credibility. Both were in a single adset with no backups and early fatigue signals. Rather than replacing them, each was iterated by isolating what was driving performance: new footage, updated scripts, adjusted aspect ratios for 1:1 and 9:16 placements. Four copy angles ran against both originals simultaneously. The structure that worked stayed intact. The variables around it were tested.
Creative

Two winners. Built out, not replaced.

When the account was taken over, two videos were already converting — one built around the transformation itself, one built around the company behind it. Both were running in a single adset with no backups. The first job was to understand what was driving each one before touching them.

That diagnosis shaped everything that came after. Every iteration was built by isolating a variable from one of those two originals — new footage holding the same structure, new script holding the same hook, new format holding the same offer. Nothing was replaced. Everything was extended.

Winner A — The Transformation Format
Original Winner A
The Reveal — Timelapse Format
The account's top performer. A cabinet refacing timelapse with before-and-after sliders and a pricing CTA. What made it work wasn't just the transformation — it was the specificity of the visual proof combined with a concrete price anchor. Homeowners could see the result and immediately know what it would cost them. Peak performance: 45.5% set rate, $463 cost-per-demo.
45.5% set rate · $463 CPD at peak
Iteration from Winner A
The Local Signal — City Callout Format
The timelapse structure held, but with one variable changed: the primary text and headline now named the city directly — Columbus, Cincinnati, Dayton — depending on which CBO the ad ran in. The hypothesis was that the original worked partly because it felt specific. Naming the city made it more specific. The county CBO running this version launched at a 48% set rate, suggesting geo-relevance was improving lead quality before the call was even made.
48% set rate on county CBO launch
Winner B — The Authority Format
Original Winner B
The Authority Play — Family-Owned Story
The second proven video. Where Winner A led with the transformation, this one led with the company — family-owned, local, accountable. Before-and-after storytelling was still present, but the trust signal came first. It was less efficient than the timelapse at peak but significantly more durable: less affected by creative lifecycle over time, and the source of 7 attributed sales. What it proved was that a different emotional hook — credibility rather than visual proof — could drive the same downstream action.
7 sales · 34.5% set rate · $562 CPD
Iteration from Winner B
The Problem First — Friction Hook
An iteration that took Winner B's credibility foundation and changed the entry point: rather than opening with the company, it opened with the homeowner's problem. "Want to upgrade your kitchen without the cost of a full remodel?" — friction named up front, offer introduced after. New footage, same authority positioning in the body. This angle showed a higher set rate than the incentive-first version, suggesting homeowners who respond to problem-framing are further along in their decision before they even submit the form.
Higher set rate than incentive angle
Cross-cutting layers — applied across both
Copy Test
The Discount Lever — Incentive Angle
One of four copy angles run against both winning videos simultaneously. Led with a 40%-off offer in the primary text. Incentive-first copy produced higher lead volume at lower CPL — which looks good until you check the set rate. More leads, fewer bookings. The pattern was consistent enough to be a signal, not noise: price-led copy attracts a different kind of homeowner than transformation-led or credibility-led copy does.
Higher volume · lower set rate vs. other angles
Retargeting Layer
The Social Carry — Post ID Retargeting
Both winning videos were served to warm audiences via their original Post IDs, carrying over months of accumulated social proof — reactions, comments, shares — rather than running as fresh creatives with zero engagement. The same ads that had already converted cold audiences now worked harder on people who'd already seen the brand, with visible proof that others had engaged. A compounding layer on top of what was already working.
Social proof carried forward on warm audiences
Results

The fixes compounded. The account scaled.

OOA dropped from 37% to 24% after geo restructuring — more than one in eight leads that had been unworkable were now in-area. The county CBO launched with a 48% set rate on per-region targeting, validating that geo-specific creative was improving lead quality before the call. As both fixes took hold, the account absorbed more spend without the downstream metrics degrading. Spend grew from $30k to $110k over four months.

What I Took From This

Lead quality issues don’t come from one place. Neither do the fixes.

1.
OOA rate is one of the most underleveraged diagnostic metrics in home improvement lead gen. It is easy to obsess over CPL while ignoring that a significant share of those leads cannot be served. Fixing the OOA problem improved the effective CPL without touching a single campaign. The number that looked fine on a dashboard was masking the number that actually mattered.
2.
City CBOs with callouts outperform DMA CBOs when city coverage is limited. Three cities with their names in the creative copy beat one DMA without them. The ability to name the city creates immediate relevance that shows up in set rate, not CPL — which is exactly why it's easy to miss if you're only watching the top of the funnel.
3.
A winning ad tells you what to build next — if you know what made it win. Diagnosing which element of the original was doing the work — the transformation visual, the price anchor, the authority positioning — made every iteration a specific test rather than a guess. That's the difference between creative depth that compounds and creative volume that just adds noise.
