Case Studies / Kitchen Remodeling

The lead count looked fine. The lead quality didn't.

A kitchen cabinet refacing account had a CPL problem rooted in out-of-area leads, a low-converting landing page, and a creative structure that hadn't kept pace with its own winning ad. Three separate fixes applied at the same time.

The Situation

The CPL problem was a symptom. Three separate constraints were causing it.

By March, 37% of leads were coming from outside the client's serviceable area. The OOA rate had grown from 31.6% in January to 33.3% in February to 37% in March. For every three leads generated, more than one was a call the sales team could not book. At higher spend levels, the account would have become unworkable.

The landing page was converting at 2.34%. For a home improvement funnel targeting homeowners actively looking to renovate, that number was suppressing what the top-of-funnel creative could genuinely deliver. Something between click and form submission was adding friction.

And the account's best-performing video, the one with a 45.5% set rate and $463 cost-per-demo at its peak, was running in only one adset with no backups ready. Creative fatigue was already showing in the data.

Core constraint

Three problems compounding each other: leads coming from the wrong places, a page that wasn't converting them, and a creative structure with no depth.

OOA (out-of-area) leads are submissions from homeowners outside the client's service geography. They inflate lead volume metrics, reduce set rate, and represent real spend with zero revenue potential.

The Approach

Each constraint had a specific cause. Each got a specific fix.

The improvement came from diagnosing precisely, not from adding more budget to a leaking system. Three parallel workstreams ran simultaneously rather than sequentially.

01
Fix the OOA problem at the structure level
Cleveland was generating leads from only 3-5 targeted zip codes but bleeding spend across a much wider metro radius. A geo-fence exclusion was applied. Beyond that, the campaign philosophy shifted toward location-first CBOs with city callouts in the creative rather than all-zips campaigns generating volume without geographic precision. A weekly OOA review cadence was established.
02
Close the ad-to-page gap
Three changes to the landing page: an urgency banner added above the fold, Indiana added to the geographic header (a meaningful share of the audience was Indiana-based but the page didn't reflect that), and the disclaimer text repositioned to reduce distraction from the CTA. All three changes went live February 21st.
03
Launch a city CBO for the top three DMAs
A DMA-level CBO would have been the natural choice but DMA targeting prevents using the city name in the creative copy. Three cities had sufficient audience size and strong historical performance: Columbus (240k, 31% of lifetime leads), Cincinnati (300k, 14% CoM), Dayton (220k, 10.8% CoM, $169 CPL). A city CBO allowed city-specific callouts in headline and primary text.
04
Build creative depth behind the winning ad
The two proven videos were iterated rather than replaced. Scripts updated with new incentives, aspect ratios adjusted to 1:1 and 9:16 to cover all placements, and new footage swapped in without changing the structural elements that drove the original performance. A copy test ran four angles against the winner simultaneously.
Creative

Iteration, not replacement.

Learning
City callout copy
Columbus, Cincinnati, and Dayton callouts in headline and primary text. The city CBO format allows the creative to speak directly to where the homeowner lives. Early results from the county CBO showed a 36% set rate, suggesting location specificity was improving lead qualification before the call.
36% set rate on county CBO after launch
Tested
40%-off incentive angle
One of four copy angles tested against the winning video. Incentive-focused copy consistently produced higher lead volume at lower CPL but weaker downstream metrics. The pattern held here: more leads, fewer sets.
Higher volume, lower set rate vs. other angles
Tested
Pain-point hook copy
"Want to upgrade your kitchen without the cost of a remodel?" Opened with the friction before pivoting to the offer. One of four angles in the copy test. Showed higher set rate than the incentive angle. Results still under evaluation at the end of the documented period.
Higher set rate than incentive angle
Learning
Post ID retargeting
Retargeting campaign used Post IDs from all-time best-performing ads, carrying over accumulated social proof (reactions, comments, shares) to warm audiences. The hypothesis was that social proof signals would increase trust for homeowners who had already seen the brand.
Leverages existing social proof on warm audiences
Results

A 45% CVR improvement delivered more leads than most campaign optimisations could.

The landing page changes went live February 21st. Within four days CVR climbed from 2.34% to 3.29%, reaching 3.40% by the following week. At the account's existing traffic volume, that improvement represented roughly 45 additional leads per month from the same budget. The county CBO launched with a 36% set rate and 5% CoM, becoming the account's primary scaling vehicle.

What I Took From This

The landing page is part of the job.

1.
OOA rate is one of the most underleveraged diagnostic metrics in home improvement lead gen. It is easy to obsess over CPL while ignoring that a significant share of those leads cannot be served. Fixing the OOA problem improved the effective CPL without touching a single campaign.
2.
Landing page CVR is part of the media buyer's job, even when it sits outside the ad account. The difference between 2.34% and 3.40% represented more incremental lead volume than most campaign-level optimisations deliver in the same period. The ad gets people to the page. The page has to close the deal.
3.
City CBOs with callouts outperform DMA CBOs when city coverage is limited. Three cities with their names in the creative copy beat one DMA without them. The ability to name the city creates immediate relevance. That relevance shows up in set rate, not CPL, which is why it's easy to miss if you're not measuring downstream.
