Case Studies / Roofing

They found their winner before they had a system to scale it.

A US roofing company running across 11 states and five market regions — Mid-Atlantic, New England, Northern New England, Northern Virginia, and South Pennsylvania — and spending $90k–$115k a month on Meta had one creative doing all the work and no architecture to support growth. Building the creative system and market structure came before scaling a single dollar.

The Situation

Strong ambition. One creative. No architecture to support scale.

The account had a clear path forward: the client projected scaling to five times the initial monthly spend across all markets within ten months. That kind of growth requires a creative pipeline that can absorb increasing spend without frequency pushing CPL to unacceptable levels. One aging video cannot do that.

The market architecture was equally underdeveloped for the budget level. The account ran primarily on broad all-zips adsets and a single state-level CBO. The two largest markets, MIDA (Mid-Atlantic) and NENG (New England), had meaningfully different performance profiles but were being managed with identical structures. MIDA and NENG needed their own campaign logic, their own budgets, and their own creative callouts.

CPL had already come down from $206 to $188 in the early weeks of active management. That was progress. But CoM% data lagged because of the roofing sales cycle: installs close weeks after the lead comes in, so February performance was not yet visible in February's numbers. The account needed to be built for six months out, not optimised for last week.

Core constraint

One creative doing the work of nine. One campaign structure managing five distinct markets. The ceiling was architectural, not tactical.

In roofing, the sales cycle from lead to closed install typically runs four to eight weeks. This means CoM% for any given month reflects leads from the prior month. Optimising to short-term CoM in this vertical produces decisions that look correct but often aren't.

The Approach

Iterate on what works. Build the architecture to scale it.

The winning video had a structural formula that had proven itself. The goal was not to replace it but to understand which of its components were driving performance, then produce enough variations of it to sustain scale across five markets and eleven states.

01
Deconstruct the winning formula
The original video had four components: a spokesperson-led hook (shingle throw), a timelapse installation sequence, a benefits-and-authority middle (30 years, 200,000+ installs, lifetime warranty), and a direct CTA with the 50%-off offer. The brief isolated each component as a variable to test: new scripts on the same footage, same script on new drone footage sequences, and hybrids swapping individual elements.
02
Build a mega brief for 11-state coverage
Each market needed versions of the winning ad with state-specific callouts in both the script voiceover and text overlay. A "mega brief" format was developed: one master template, nine variants, structured so the editor could produce market-specific versions efficiently rather than treating each state as a separate production.
03
Give each market its own campaign architecture
MIDA, NENG, NHME, NOVA, and SOPA each received a dedicated monthly budget and an ABO structure with one broad adset (top Post IDs) and one market-specific adset (highest-performing zip codes by lifetime CPL and close rate). County-level CBOs were queued for MIDA and NENG once the all-zips data had built enough signal.
04
Introduce the 3-requirements qualifier in tight markets
NENG and NHME had tight serviceable zip code lists. The 3-requirements format, a proven angle across multiple home improvement accounts, was introduced as a video concept for these markets. A qualifying hook pre-filters for in-area homeowners before they reach the landing page, improving set rate without changing CPL.

The brief was structured to isolate variables, not reinvent the wheel. Each variant changed one thing, so performance differences could be attributed to the specific element being tested.

Results

CPL down 21%. The account was built to absorb 5x spend. That was the goal.

CPL dropped from $206 to approximately $162 over four months. Set rates held above 27% in NENG and MIDA. The creative library moved from one aging video to nine tested variants with enough rotation to sustain increasing spend levels without frequency degradation. CoM% held at 13.7% through January.

What I Took From This

Scale reveals the gaps in your creative system.

1.
A single winning creative is a starting point, not a system. The goal of early account management is to understand why the winner works structurally, then build a methodology to produce more of them. Isolating individual variables across multiple video variants is slower than launching entirely new concepts. It produces more reliable winners because each iteration is grounded in what already converted.
2.
Multi-market accounts need market-level architecture from the start. Running MIDA and NENG inside the same broad campaigns, despite different performance profiles, was the single biggest structural limitation on scale. Separating them made the account simpler to manage, not more complex, because each market's performance became readable on its own terms.
3.
Roofing CoM% should be measured in trailing 60-90 day windows. Optimising to monthly CoM in an account with a six to eight week sales cycle produces decisions that look coherent at the top of the funnel but are wrong for the business. Patience in reading the data is a strategic input, not a passive posture.
