Creative Scorecard · Lead Gen + DTC

When to cut, scale, hold, or extend

Every ad decision in a live Meta account comes down to four possible actions. This scorecard makes the criteria explicit — the thresholds, the time windows, and the signal that separates a temporary dip from a structural underperformer. The goal is to remove intuition from decisions that have enough data to be systematic.

Applies to Lead Gen · DTC
Platform Meta (FB + IG)
Decision types Cut · Scale · Hold · Extend
Review cadence 14-day + 28-day windows
The principle

The filter only works if you apply it without exceptions

Every account has ads that feel like they're about to turn around. The creative team briefed them carefully. The hook looked promising in the static test. It's been live for ten days and the trend looks like it's moving. The rule is: if an ad fails two or more thresholds across both the 14-day and 28-day windows, cut it. Not next week. Not after one more data point. The accounts that consistently over-held underperformers spent weeks of budget on ads that never recovered. The ones that cut on threshold recovered that budget into validated performers and moved faster.

The exception logic matters too. A new ad under 500 impressions doesn't have enough data. A vertical with a long sales cycle needs its success metrics shifted to set rate and cost-per-demo, not CPL alone. These aren't loopholes — they're built into the scorecard.

Decision sequence

The questions, in order

These run in sequence. Skipping to question 3 without answering 1 and 2 is how accounts end up cutting good creative and keeping bad structure.

1
Does this ad have enough data to evaluate?
Minimum: 500 impressions, 7 days live. Below this threshold the data is noise, not signal. No cut decision is valid before this point regardless of what the numbers look like.
Yes → continue No → wait
2
Is this a creative problem or a structure problem?
Check: are other ads in the same adset performing normally? If yes, the problem is the creative. If no, the problem may be structure — audience overlap, budget competition, or a broken adset. Don't cut creative to fix a structure problem.
Creative → score it Structure → fix first
3
How many scorecard thresholds does it fail?
Run the scorecard across the 14-day and 28-day windows. One threshold failure = hold and monitor. Two or more failures across both windows = cut. No exceptions for ads that feel promising or were recently briefed.
0–1 fails → hold 2+ fails → cut
4
If passing — is it ready to scale or extend?
Passing all thresholds across both windows = validated winner. Check frequency in the primary demographic. Below 2.5 and holding → scale budget. Above 3.5 and trending up → brief extensions now before frequency forces the decision.
Low freq → scale High freq → extend
5
Does the vertical have a long sales cycle?
In roofing, windows, bath remodeling — lead to close is 4–8 weeks. CoM% and cost-per-demo trail CPL by that window. Evaluate CPL and set rate on 14–28 day windows. Evaluate CoM% on trailing 60–90 days only. A good month can look bad because last month's leads are still closing.
Yes → use trailing No → standard
Built-in exceptions

When the scorecard needs context

New account / no history
First 60 days: evaluate by concept category, not individual ad
A new account is training the algorithm. Cutting aggressively in the first 60 days removes the signal the account needs to learn. In this window, evaluate creative by concept type — problem-aware vs. solution-aware, candid vs. direct response — and cut only the category if the entire concept approach is failing. Don't cut individual ads with under 1,000 impressions.
Duplicate lead contamination
Fix exclusions before reading set rate
A set rate below threshold can mean bad creative or it can mean the same lead is entering the funnel multiple times and inflating volume. Before cutting on set rate, verify the exclusion lists are current. In the windows and doors case, 23% of "leads" were duplicates — fixing exclusions improved set rate without touching the creative at all.
Long sales cycle verticals
Never optimize CoM% monthly in roofing or windows
The month's CoM% reflects leads from 4–8 weeks prior. January's number is December's creative. Cutting in January based on January CoM% is cutting based on the wrong period. Always evaluate CoM% in trailing 60–90 day windows and note which month's leads are actually closing that month.
Geographic budget concentration
Don't read geo-specific ads against account-level benchmarks
A state-specific ad running in a tight-zip region will have a different CPL than the same ad running broad. Evaluate it against the market's historical CPL, not the account average. The bath remodeling account had state-level CPLs ranging from $50 to $120 — the right benchmark for each ad was the state it was running in, not the aggregate.
