Creative Lifecycle Hub

The creative system in four parts.

Four layers. Each one solves a different problem. None of them do what the others do.

Research reads the market before anything gets briefed. Reviews, competitor gaps, past performance, customer language. All of it processed into angles and personas with a traceable source. The raw material.

Coverage takes that material and audits how it sits across the account. What personas are missing creative. Which angles have been over-tested. What the account is telling Meta versus what it should be.

Hooks decide how each individual ad enters its territory. The hook type is an audience selection decision, not a creative one. Different hook types reach different buyer segments at different stages of awareness.

Lifecycle manages what happens to a signal once it starts working. Discovery, validation, expansion, saturation, decay. The goal is to extend the signal before decay forces the account into emergency creative mode.

How the system produces creative
The insight gate
Four questions. All four must clear.

If any answer is no, what you have is an observation. Go back to the language bank.

01
Can you name a person in a specific situation?
Not a demographic. A moment — the circumstance that makes this true for them right now.
02
Can you name what they tell themselves to avoid acting?
The rationalization — not the objection they'd say out loud, but the internal story that makes delay feel reasonable.
03
Can you name the belief that makes inaction feel rational?
The frame underneath the rationalization. What they'd have to stop believing for the ad to land.
04
Can you name a consequence they're not connecting to the cause?
Something already happening because of the stuck belief — that they haven't traced back to the real problem.
Five worked examples — raw material to insight
Raw material "It's not leaking yet." / "I'll deal with it after the holidays." / "I got a quote two years ago and it was way more than I expected." / "I keep meaning to call someone."
Shaped insight A homeowner who can see visible shingle deterioration tells himself the roof "still has a few years left" — not because he believes it, but because a contractor quote feels like the start of an expense he can't control. So the problem stays dormant until the first interior water stain. Even then, he calls for a patch.
01 · Person + situation
Homeowner with visible deterioration — the moment he notices but files it away
02 · Rationalization
"Still has a few years left" — delay reframed as responsible judgement
03 · Stuck belief
A quote = committing to an expense he can't control
04 · Unconnected consequence
He calls for a patch when water appears — spending more eventually while believing he was being careful
Psychological mechanism: Loss aversion applied to the process itself, not the product. The threat isn't the roof — it's the loss of financial control the quote represents.
Raw material "I use the guest bathroom when I can." / "I've been meaning to redo it for five years." / "I don't want strangers in my house for weeks." / "Every contractor I've called never follows up."
Shaped insight A homeowner who has mentally accepted her bathroom is outdated stops short of booking a consultation — not because she can't afford it, but because every past attempt to hire a contractor ended in silence. She's reframed the project as "not worth the hassle of being ignored again." Her bathroom stays untouched, and the fixture she hates most becomes the thing she just stops seeing.
01 · Person + situation
Homeowner who's been through the quoting process before — and got nowhere
02 · Rationalization
"Not worth the hassle" — inaction framed as self-protection, not avoidance
03 · Stuck belief
The process itself is broken — the product isn't the problem, the category is
04 · Unconnected consequence
Adaptive blindness — she stops seeing the problem. Five years become ten without active decision.
Psychological mechanism: Learned helplessness. Past failure conditions the response to future opportunity. The hook can't sell the product — it has to first rehabilitate the category.
Raw material "I just use whatever's in the shower." / "I tried a few things but nothing stuck." / "My wife buys stuff for me sometimes." / "I don't really have a routine."
Shaped insight A man in his mid-30s who is quietly aware that how he looks affects how he's perceived at work uses "I'm not really a grooming person" as his cover story — because building a routine feels like admitting he's been letting himself go. He buys nothing deliberately. His wife occasionally buys something for him, which he uses until it runs out, then reverts.
01 · Person + situation
Mid-30s professional — aware of appearance stakes, not acting on them
02 · Rationalization
"I'm not a grooming person" — indifference performed as identity
03 · Stuck belief
Starting a routine = admitting prior neglect, which threatens his self-image
04 · Unconnected consequence
Passive consumption — someone else makes every purchase decision, nothing compounds, no loyalty forms
Psychological mechanism: Identity protection. The barrier isn't price or awareness — it's that purchase implies acknowledgement of a gap he hasn't admitted. The hook has to give him a story that doesn't require confession.
Raw material "My garage floor looks like a gas station." / "I always just park the car and close the door." / "I didn't think it was something you could actually fix without tearing everything up." / "I thought it was too expensive."
Shaped insight A homeowner with a cracked, oil-stained garage floor has stopped seeing it — not because it doesn't bother him, but because he filed it under "structural" in his mind, placing it in the same mental category as foundation work. Expensive. Disruptive. Not something you just call someone about. So he closes the door. The one space he could actually own stays the one he avoids.
01 · Person + situation
Homeowner who's noticed the floor, disliked it, and adapted to avoid it
02 · Rationalization
"Too expensive / too disruptive" — closes the door, problem filed as permanent
03 · Stuck belief
Garage floor = structural = same category as foundation repair. Out of reach by definition.
04 · Unconnected consequence
The space goes unused or underused. A fixable problem becomes a permanent limitation because of a categorization error.
Psychological mechanism: Miscategorization. The product is easy and affordable — the buyer just has it in the wrong mental folder. The hook's only job is to move it.
Raw material "I've tried so many things." / "I don't know if it actually does anything or if I'm just feeling better." / "I stopped taking it when I ran out and meant to reorder." / "I forget to take it most days."
Shaped insight A buyer who genuinely felt better on a supplement stops reordering after the first bottle runs out — not because it didn't work, but because feeling better removed the urgency that made her buy it in the first place. She tells herself she'll reorder "when she needs it again." She never does. The improvement quietly reverses, and she attributes that to something else.
01 · Person + situation
First-time buyer at the end of her first bottle — genuinely improved, not yet churned
02 · Rationalization
"I'll reorder when I need it again" — responsible framing for what is passive inaction
03 · Stuck belief
Feeling good = problem solved = product no longer needed. The relief deletes the memory of the problem.
04 · Unconnected consequence
Churn that looks like disengagement — she declines slowly, attributes it to other causes, never reconnects the dots back to stopping
Psychological mechanism: Success-induced amnesia. The product works so well it eliminates the felt need that drove purchase. Retention creative has to make the before-state vivid again before it fades completely.
The production cadence
12–20 distinct ads per cycle

The coverage map decides the volume. Count the genuine gaps, brief each with 2–3 hook variants. What stays fixed is the rhythm — two weeks from brief to live, every cycle, without interruption.

Days 1–2
Brief
One per ad. Angle cites its source. Persona named with decision context.
Days 3–7
Production
Batched by format. AI compresses what it fits. The map doesn't change.
Days 8–11
Edit
Hook variants as separate files — not swaps inside one timeline.
Days 12–13
Ship
Consistent naming so cell-level reading stays possible after launch.
Day 14
Restart
Next cycle begins the day this one ships. The account never stops.
How the pieces connect

One question per layer.
No layer repeats another.

Research reads the market and surfaces the angles, personas, and hook language that go into the brief. It is the only layer that works from external signal rather than account data.

Coverage takes that material and maps it across the account. Which personas have creative. Which angles are over-served. What needs to exist before the next round gets briefed. Coverage works from the inside out.

Hooks decide how each ad opens the territory coverage identified. The hook type is an audience selection decision. Change the hook type and you change which buyer stops scrolling, regardless of what the rest of the ad says.

Lifecycle manages a winning ad through its predictable stages. The goal is extension before decay, not reaction after it. When the cycle closes, its data feeds back into research and coverage, and the process starts again.
