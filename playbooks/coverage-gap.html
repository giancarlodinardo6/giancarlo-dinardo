The gaps aren't just missing ads — they're audiences the algorithm can't reach
The problem

The coverage problem is invisible until you map it.

There's a version of this problem that's obvious: an account with three ads and no testing. Everyone recognizes that. The harder version to see is the account with forty ads, healthy production volume, consistent testing cadence — and still hitting a ceiling. CPMs climbing. Frequency building. Incremental reach declining.

The instinct is to produce more. Brief another batch. Try a new format. But forty ads covering two psychological territories is effectively the same as two ads. Meta's algorithm groups similar creative by entity ID and delivers them to the same audience cluster. The reach was never growing because the creative wasn't covering new ground.

⚑ Confirm observation When I've audited accounts that are hitting frequency walls, the coverage map almost always shows the same thing: one or two personas well-served, a couple of consistent angles, everything else a gap. The ads exist. The budget is being spent. But the territory is a fraction of what's available.

~20%
⚑ Confirm figure The share of possible creative territory most accounts are actually covering when mapped honestly against personas and angles
The rest of the grid is gaps. Most of them aren't intentional — they're territory that was never mapped and therefore never briefed.
The distinction

Coverage isn't the number of ads running. It's the number of distinct territories those ads are built for.

Two ads addressing the same persona at the same awareness level with the same emotional angle aren't two creative directions. They're one direction with a visual variation. Meta treats them that way. Running ten versions of the same concept doesn't expand reach — it deepens penetration into a segment that's already saturating.

Real coverage means each ad is built for a different person, in a different emotional state, at a different point in their decision. Those combinations generate distinct signals. Those signals let the algorithm find genuinely different people.

Not coverage
Ten ads with different hooks but the same persona, awareness level, and emotional angle
Not coverage
The same message in different formats — UGC version and static version of the same concept
Coverage
An ad built for a problem-aware persona and a separate ad built for a solution-aware persona with the same product
Coverage
An emotional angle for one persona and an authority-led angle for a different persona in the same macro segment
Why it matters to Meta

"Andromeda doesn't care how many ads you have running. It cares how many distinct psychological territories those ads are covering."

When ads look similar — same persona signals, same emotional register, same visual environment — Meta groups them as one entity and delivers them to the same audience cluster. More volume of the same creative doesn't give the algorithm more territory to find. It just gives it more of the same brief at higher cost.

The audit

How to map your coverage

The coverage map is a simple grid. Personas on one axis, angles on the other. For each combination, mark whether you have creative running that was explicitly built for that persona at that angle. Not creative that could reach them — creative that was briefed specifically for them.

Gaps are audience segments the algorithm has never received a signal for

1
List your active personas
Not demographic groups — psychological profiles. Who is this person, what do they believe about the problem, and where are they in their decision?
2
List your active angles
Emotional, authority, functional, comparison, contrarian, problem-first, aspiration. Each is a genuinely different creative direction, not a format variation.
3
Be honest about what's actually running
For each cell: is there creative running that was explicitly built for this combination? If it was built for a different persona and happens to reach this one, that's a gap.
4
Prioritize gaps, don't chase completeness
Not every cell is worth filling. The goal is a deliberate map, not a full one. You decide which gaps represent real audience opportunity based on where the business case is.
Prioritization

Not every gap is worth filling. Here's how to choose which ones are.

The map shows you what's missing. Prioritization decides what to build next. Three types of gaps tend to be worth addressing first.

→
Adjacent persona gaps
Look at which personas you're already winning with and ask what's directly adjacent. Same product, different emotional entry point, different audience the algorithm can find.
⚑ Example from the creative strategy map: if the anxious procrastinator is converting well in a roofing account, the value-conscious planner is the next territory worth covering.
↕
Systematic angle gaps
Which angles are completely absent across all personas? If you're running emotional and authority-based creative but nothing comparison or contrarian, that's a systematic gap from a brief template that defaults to the same angle type every time.
Systematic gaps are usually invisible until you map — they never get noticed because nothing in the account points at them.
↑
Awareness level gaps
⚑ Confirm Most accounts over-index on product-aware creative because that's what converts in the short term. Unaware and problem-aware creative is harder to attribute but it's what expands the audience pool.
The awareness level question is the most overlooked one — the gap is often the largest here precisely because short-term attribution doesn't reward filling it.

With the coverage map

The ceiling lifts — not because production volume increased, but because what was produced started covering more ground
