The premise

Creative does the targeting. Most accounts haven't worked out what that costs them.

Under broad delivery, Meta uses your creative as the primary signal for audience finding. The angle you lead with, the persona you speak to, the emotional register of the opening — all of it tells the algorithm which kind of person to go find. That's the foundation the Creative Strategy Map is built on. What most accounts don't work out is what happens at the budget level when the creative isn't genuinely diverse: spend concentrates into a narrow segment, similar ads get collapsed into the same delivery entity, and the addressable market you're actually reaching is a fraction of what's available. More creative doesn't fix it. More distinct creative does. That's what this piece is about.

Three consequences — covered in depth below
01 — Budget concentrates in one segment
Meta's auction is winner-take-most. A marginal performance edge produces a disproportionate budget share. New creative gets starved before the algorithm has enough signal to evaluate it.
02 — Similar ads share the same entity ID
Andromeda groups creatives that look or sound similar into the same delivery entity. They share reach and fatigue the same segment together. A different headline doesn't escape this. A different concept does.
03 — Reach is narrower than you think
Different creative reaches different people — not just different segments of the same audience, but categorically different buyers. Running one concept type means leaving entire pockets of the market untouched regardless of how many ads are live.
The mechanics

What's actually happening inside the auction

Most accounts know creative matters. Fewer understand the specific structural reasons why lack of diversity costs them at the budget and reach level. These three mechanisms are what separate accounts that scale cleanly from accounts that plateau — and none of them are platform guidelines. They're features of how the auction and delivery systems work that don't change regardless of what Meta recommends.

Auction mechanics
01
Winner-take-most budget allocation
Meta's CBO system isn't proportional — it's winner-take-most. The ad that's performing even marginally better gets a disproportionate share of budget. This means ads that haven't had a real chance to spend get cut off before the algorithm has enough signal to evaluate them fairly. Separate campaigns and minimum spend limits are the structural fix, not creative changes.
Delivery system
02
Andromeda's similarity detection
Meta's Andromeda ranking system groups ads that share visual or audio similarity — particularly in the first three seconds — into the same delivery entity. They share reach, share fatigue, and share whatever the algorithm has learned about that creative territory. The threshold is lower than most people expect: same creator, same setting, same opening frame is enough to trigger it. True creative diversity means different concept, different visual world, different hook.
Audience finding
03
Creative as the targeting signal
The emotional register, the persona being addressed, the problem being named in the first three seconds — all of it tells the algorithm which kind of person to find. Different hook types reach genuinely different buyer segments, not just different slices of the same one. The Hooks Are Targeting playbook covers this mechanism in full, including a five-type taxonomy and worked examples. The implication for diversity: running one hook type across all your creative means reaching one audience type, regardless of how many ads are live.
The framework

Three axes of meaningful diversity.

Not all creative variation is meaningful to the algorithm or to buyers. Changing a headline, swapping a colour, cutting a different version of the same video — none of that creates the kind of diversity that opens new audience segments. These are the three axes where genuine differentiation happens, and where the creative decisions you make before production actually determine reach at scale.

01
Axis of diversity
Concept — the angle and emotional entry point
The core idea behind the ad: what problem it names, what desire it addresses, what belief it challenges. Two ads about the same product with different emotional entry points are genuinely different in the algorithm's eyes. They reach genuinely different people. This is where the creative strategy map does its work.
Meaningful variation
Pain-point angle vs. social proof angle vs. transformation angle — same product, three distinct concepts, three different audience segments found
02
Axis of diversity
Format — the visual and production style
Static and video reach different users on Meta — not just different preferences, but demonstrably different people. UGC-style content and polished studio content find different segments. The production register of the ad is itself a targeting signal. Running only one format means your creative diversity is capped regardless of how many concepts you have.
Meaningful variation
Lo-fi UGC vs. polished static vs. creator-shot video — same concept, different formats, different users reached by each
03
Axis of diversity
Persona — who the ad is speaking to
An ad written for someone who's never heard of the product reaches different people than one written for someone actively comparing options. Speaking to a first-time buyer vs. someone replacing a product they already own produces different delivery. The awareness level and persona of the ad determines which stage of the buying journey Meta looks for. Those populations don't overlap cleanly.
Meaningful variation
Unaware audience hook vs. solution-aware comparison ad vs. most-aware offer ad — same product, three buyer stages, three distinct pools
Worked example

What this looks like inside an account

At Browze, the products that scaled most cleanly were never the ones with the best single creative. They were the ones where we had genuinely different concepts running simultaneously — different angles, different formats, different buyer stages — so that when the algorithm went looking for buyers, it had multiple distinct signals to work with. The scenario below represents how budget distribution played out across a home and lifestyle product at around $4k/day in spend. The concept labels and percentages are illustrative; the pattern repeated across categories.

How to apply this

Building a diverse creative portfolio

Diversity doesn't mean producing more ads. It means producing the right spread of ads across concepts, formats, and buyer stages — then giving each one the structural conditions to actually spend and find its audience.

01
Audit your current creative for real variation
Pull your active ads and ask honestly: how many distinct concepts are running? Not variations, not format swaps — genuinely different angles with different emotional entry points. Most accounts have far fewer than they think. If everything is problem-solution with slightly different copy, you have one concept.
02
Map gaps across the three axes
Check your creative against concept, format, and persona. If every ad is video UGC speaking to a problem-aware buyer, you have gaps in format, in concept variety, and in buyer stage coverage. Brief into those gaps specifically — not just "we need more creative" but "we need a static social proof ad for a solution-aware audience."
03
Separate concepts structurally in the account
Distinct concepts need distinct ad sets with minimum spend limits — otherwise Meta's auction will pick a winner and starve the rest before they've found their audience. Structure is what gives diverse creative room to breathe. Without it, even genuinely different concepts end up competing for the same slice of budget and the same slice of audience.
04
Read performance by concept, not by ad
When you evaluate what's working, look at concept-level patterns, not individual ad results. A concept that's getting traction across two formats is a signal worth expanding. One great video and three failed statics might all be the same underlying concept — understand what the algorithm is responding to before you iterate.

Lots of ads, very few distinct signals — Meta reaches the same audience segment on repeat
Budget concentrates in one concept while the rest get starved before they find their audience
Frequency climbs in a narrow band, CPMs rise, and the account hits a ceiling that feels like a creative problem

Scale requires constantly finding new creative rather than unlocking new audiences with what you have
With intentional diversity
Each concept reaches a distinct buyer segment — the same budget covers more of the addressable market
Minimum spend limits give every concept a fair chance before the auction picks a winner
When one concept fatigues, others are already running into fresh audience — no hard reset required
The algorithm has multiple distinct signals to optimise against, which compounds over time
Scale becomes a function of concept breadth and budget, not a search for the one perfect ad
