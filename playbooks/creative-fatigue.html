Playbooks / Creative Fatigue

Most accounts misread their creative data. Here's how to fix that.

A high hook rate doesn't mean a good ad. A long-running ad doesn't mean a winning one. The metrics you're watching can lie — not because the data is wrong, but because the question behind it is.

The problem

The data is fine. The questions are wrong.

Ads Manager gives you a lot of numbers. The problem isn't access to data — it's that most people ask the wrong questions of it, and the wrong questions produce confident but misleading answers.

The most common version of this I saw working across DTC and lead gen accounts was evaluating creative against the wrong benchmark. An ad would get compared to the overall account average — which includes creatives from three months ago, different seasons, different spend levels — and get labelled a winner or a loser based on a comparison that doesn't hold. The right benchmark is what else was live in the same two-week window at a similar spend level. Everything else is noise.

The second version is conflating attention with intent. A high hook rate tells you the first three seconds stopped someone mid-scroll. It says nothing about whether that person was ever going to buy. Running roofing lead gen, a broad hook like "is your roof ready for winter?" stops a lot of people — homeowners, renters, people in apartments, people who clicked out of curiosity. A hook that says "if you've had a leak in the last 12 months" stops fewer people, but the ones who stop are in the market. For high-consideration purchases like home services, a lower hook rate from a specific hook often produces better qualified leads at a lower cost than a high hook rate from a broad one.

The fix isn't more metrics. It's understanding what each metric is actually measuring, what it can't tell you, and how to sequence the questions so you arrive at the right diagnosis before you make a creative decision.

Why these misreads are so persistent
01 — We compare against the wrong control
Account-wide averages shift constantly with spend, season, and creative mix. A new ad launched in Q4 against a Q2 average is essentially unmeasured. Rolling 2-week benchmarks by creative type give you a real control — not a historical average that no longer reflects current conditions.
02 — We treat individual metrics as verdicts
Hook rate, hold rate, CTR, and CVR are each measuring a different moment in the viewer's journey. A creative with a strong hook and weak hold rate has a specific, diagnosable problem — not a general "bad ad" problem. Sequencing these metrics tells you what to fix; reading them in isolation just tells you something is off.
03 — Personal opinion enters the analysis
The instinct to keep ads running because they look good, or kill them because they don't, is everywhere. Separating aesthetic preference from performance evaluation is harder than it sounds. The accounts that do it well consistently outperform the ones that don't.
The blind spots

Four misreads that cost accounts money

These aren't edge cases. They show up consistently across accounts of different sizes, different verticals, different teams. Each one produces a confident-sounding conclusion that turns out to be wrong. Each one has a different correct reading.

01
The assumption
"This ad has been running for three months — it must be a winner."
Longevity means nothing without spend context.
Ads stay live for all kinds of reasons — someone forgot to pause them, they're running at $1 a day out of habit, or they're held on for competitive camouflage. An ad that's been running for months at negligible spend hasn't proven anything. The only thing that matters is what it cost to produce results at meaningful spend, compared to other creatives in the same period at similar budget levels.
02
The assumption
"The hook rate is 65% — this is a strong creative."
Capturing lots of attention means nothing if it's the wrong attention.
Hook rate measures whether the first three seconds stopped someone. It doesn't measure whether the right someone stopped. A hook that says "you won't believe this product" stops scrollers broadly — including everyone who will never buy. A hook that says "if you own a home in a flood zone" stops fewer people, but far more of the right ones. For high-ticket or high-consideration purchases, a lower hook rate from a specific hook often outperforms a high hook rate from a broad one.
03
The assumption
"It looks great. The team loves it. Let's scale it."
The ad you love is not always the ad that converts.
There's a specific creative trap I've seen at every level of spend: the polished, well-produced ad that the whole team is proud of, that gets plenty of engagement, that looks exactly like what an ad should look like. It quietly underperforms the scrappy UGC variation nobody was excited about. Engagement and revenue intent are different signals. If the evaluation criteria is "does this look good" or "would I save this to my swipe file," that's not a creative strategy. That's a preference.
04
The assumption
"CTR is dropping — this creative is done."
CTR decline has a specific cause. Find it before you kill the ad.
Declining CTR often gets treated as a death sentence for a creative when it's actually a specific symptom with a specific cause. If hook rate is also dropping, the problem is in the opening — the concept may still be valid with a new hook. If hook rate holds but CTR drops, the problem is in the body or the offer — those are testable fixes. Killing the whole creative before diagnosing where the drop is happening wastes whatever the ad already taught you.
The framework

Read the metrics in sequence, not in isolation.

Each metric captures a different moment in the viewer's journey — from whether they stopped, to whether they stayed, to whether they clicked, to whether they bought. Reading them in order tells you exactly where the creative is losing people. Reading them individually gives you a number without a diagnosis. The table below shows what each metric is measuring, what a problem signal looks like, and what to do about it.

Metric What it measures Problem signal What it means Action
Hook rate
(3-sec video views / impressions)		 Whether the first three seconds stopped the scroll Below category avg The opening isn't earning attention — concept or visual execution problem Test new hooks against the same body. Different opening, same offer.
Hold rate
(15s views / 3s views)		 Whether what follows the hook keeps people watching Hook rate high, hold rate low Hook is stopping the wrong people — or the body doesn't deliver on what the hook promised Tighten the body to match hook's promise. Or test a more targeted hook that self-selects better.
CTR
(outbound clicks / impressions)		 Whether the ad created enough interest to act Hold rate ok, CTR low Offer or CTA isn't landing — the creative holds attention but fails to create urgency Test different offers or CTA framing. The concept is valid; the close isn't working.
CVR
(purchases / clicks)		 Whether the people who clicked were actually buyers CTR strong, CVR low Ad is attracting interest from people who aren't buyers — audience or message mismatch Revisit who the ad is speaking to. The creative may be working; the landing page or the audience may not be.
Frequency
(impressions / reach)		 How often the same person is seeing the ad Above 2.5 with declining CVR Audience is saturated — the ad has run out of new people to find in this segment New creative needed, not more budget. Audience has seen this enough.
Worked example

Diagnosing three creatives at once

This is the kind of situation that came up regularly across the home services lead gen accounts I managed — three ads running simultaneously, each with a different metric profile, each requiring a different response. The specifics are representative of the diagnostic patterns I saw across roofing and bathroom remodel accounts. The actual numbers would vary; the sequencing logic is exactly how I'd read them.

Ad A — Hook problem
11%
Hook rate · below avg
Hold rate: strong CTR: above avg
The hook isn't stopping enough people. But the ones who do stop are converting well — hold rate and CTR are both healthy. The concept is working. The opening isn't.
Action: keep the body, test 3 new hook variants. The concept has proven itself — don't kill the ad, replace the door.
Ad B — Body problem
58%
Hook rate · strong
Hold rate: low CTR: below avg
Hook is stopping people broadly — maybe too broadly. The body isn't holding them. This is either the wrong audience being attracted by an unspecific hook, or the body failing to deliver on what the hook promised.
Action: test a more specific hook that self-selects better, or rebuild the body to match the hook's implied promise more tightly.
Ad C — Fatigue signal
3.1
Frequency · climbing
CVR: declining CTR: holding
CTR is still fine — people are still clicking. But CVR is dropping as frequency climbs. This segment has seen the ad enough times that the people left in the pool are less and less likely to convert. The creative is not the problem.
Action: the concept is exhausted in this segment. New creative needed — not an iteration of this one. A hook swap won't fix audience saturation.
How to run this

The weekly creative review

This doesn't need to be a long process. At Browze and across the lead gen accounts I ran, a structured weekly creative review produced better decisions than ad hoc checks throughout the day — because the sequence forced a diagnosis before a verdict. The discipline is in running the same questions in the same order every time, not in how long it takes.

01
Set your benchmark window before you look at anything
Pull performance for the last 14 days only, filtered by creative type. Compare each ad against others of the same type launched in the same window — not against the account average, not against all-time figures. A static ad gets benchmarked against other statics. A UGC video against other UGC videos. Only then does the comparison mean anything.
02
Run the metric sequence top to bottom
Hook rate first. If it's below benchmark, that's the diagnosis — don't go further until you've noted it. If hook rate is fine, check hold rate. If hold rate drops, that's your diagnosis. If both hold, check CTR. If CTR holds, check CVR. The first metric that breaks from benchmark tells you where the creative is losing people. Everything downstream of that break is a symptom, not a cause.
03
Check frequency before you kill anything
Before you conclude a creative is dead, check frequency. If frequency is above 2.5 and CVR is declining, audience saturation is the most likely explanation — not creative failure. That's a different problem with a different solution. Pausing the ad and adding net-new creative to find fresh audiences is the right move, not iterating on the existing piece.
04
Separate the verdict from the action
A creative that's dying because of a hook problem is a different situation than one dying because of audience saturation — even if the CPA looks the same in both cases. The first warrants a hook test. The second warrants a full concept refresh. Making the right call depends on knowing which problem you actually have, not just that performance is declining.
Without structured diagnosis
Ads killed or scaled based on a single metric — hook rate, CTR, or ROAS — without understanding what drove it
Creative decisions made on account-wide averages that don't reflect current conditions
Long-running ads assumed to be winners without checking whether they're actually spending
Audience saturation misread as creative fatigue — leading to more iterations of a concept that's already exhausted in the segment
Team preference driving decisions that data should be making
With structured diagnosis
Each creative gets a specific verdict: hook problem, body problem, offer problem, or audience saturation — each with a different fix
Performance evaluated against a rolling 14-day benchmark by creative type — the only comparison that's actually meaningful
Creative decisions separated from aesthetic preference — what converts vs. what looks good stops being the same question
Concepts salvaged through hook or body fixes rather than killed prematurely — protecting whatever learning has already accumulated
Saturation spotted before it's misread as failure — so the response is new creative, not more iterations of the same thing
