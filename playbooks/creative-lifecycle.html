Playbook · Lifecycle

Winning creatives don't last. They evolve.

Every winner has a half-life. The job isn't to find one and ride it until it craters. It's to read where it is in its lifecycle and extend the signal into something new before the decay shows up in the numbers.

By Giancarlo Di Nardo · 5 min read

Decay is a schedule, not a surprise

When a strong ad starts to slip, the instinct is to wait and see, then panic. By the time CPL has moved, frequency has already been climbing for weeks. Treat fatigue as something you plan for. The moment a creative is clearly winning is the moment to start briefing its successors, while you still have budget, signal and time on your side.

Extend the winner before it decays, don't replace it after it has.

Evolve the signal, don't restart it

A new ad doesn't have to mean a blank page. The fastest, safest way to extend a winner is to change one element at a time: a new script on the same footage, the same script on fresh footage, a new hook in front of a proven body. Each variation keeps the part that's already converting and tests the part that might extend its life. That's how one formula became nine attribution-clean variants on the roofing account.

Know which question each variant answers

Lifecycle work only compounds if it stays diagnostic. When you isolate the variable you're changing, every result tells you something: the hook was carrying it, or the message was, or the footage had simply gone stale. That knowledge is the real output. It's what lets the next brief make a genuinely different bet instead of producing one more lookalike of an ad that's already on its way down.
