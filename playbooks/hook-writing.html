The allocation problem

You spend the most time on the part of the ad that matters least.

A typical creative brief runs several pages. The script has scene directions, dialogue, b-roll notes. The offer gets careful framing. The CTA gets A/B tested. The hook gets a sentence at the top, usually written last, usually by whoever is doing the brief rather than whoever is best at copy.

The Pareto logic is simple: the first three seconds determine whether anything that follows gets seen. If the hook fails, the scriptwriting doesn't matter, the actor you hired doesn't matter, the offer framing doesn't matter. None of it runs. The viewer is gone before the ad has started.

This isn't an argument for spending less time on the body. It's an argument for treating hook-writing as a primary creative act rather than a preamble to one. The hook deserves its own brief. Its own iteration. Its own testing cycle before anything goes to production. Treating it as the most important creative decision, rather than the first line of the brief, changes how you allocate time, how you structure testing, and ultimately what you ship.

At a DTC home and lifestyle brand, producing a high volume of creative every week, this reordering changed how briefs got structured. Hooks got written and debated before scripts were touched. The body came after the direction was clear. It's slower at the front and faster overall, because you stop building full productions on openings that haven't been tested.

Why the brief allocation gets it backwards
01, The hook is written after the concept
Most briefs start with the concept and work toward the hook. That's the wrong direction. The hook determines who stops, which determines who sees the concept at all. Writing the concept first and bolting a hook on top means the hook is shaped by the script rather than the other way around, and the script is usually written for the wrong audience.
02, Hook quality is treated as a copy problem
Hook-writing gets treated as copywriting, a skill applied at the end of the creative process, not at the beginning of the strategic one. A strong hook requires knowing exactly who you're speaking to, what they're feeling at the moment they see the ad, and what reaction you're trying to force. That's a strategic brief, not a copy task.
03, The visual half of the hook gets no brief at all
A hook is a word-and-image combination. The visual registers before the words do. If the opening frame looks like an ad, branded, polished, promotional, the viewer's guard is up before the line has a chance to land. The visual brief for the hook deserves as much attention as the copy brief. Most creative processes don't have one.
The framework

Three ingredients.
A hook needs at least one.

A hook doesn't need all three to work, but it needs at least one to have any chance. Hooks that fail usually fail because they have none: they describe the product, introduce the brand, or set up context without creating relevance, curiosity, or any emotional friction. The three ingredients below aren't formulas. They're diagnostic questions you ask before you write the first line.

01
Ingredient
Relevance
Does the hook speak directly to this person's real problem, desire, or belief?
Relevance hooks work by making the right person feel immediately seen. They name a specific situation, condition, or belief that only a certain buyer would have, and that specificity is the mechanism. The people who aren't the target feel nothing and scroll. The people who are the target feel like the ad was written for them, which is exactly what you want before you've said a word about the product.
Example
"Your toes will never be cold again." Simple. Specific. The right person stops immediately.
02
Ingredient
Curiosity
Does the viewer feel like they'll miss something important if they scroll past?
Curiosity hooks open a loop the viewer can't close without watching. The mechanism introduced later in the ad, the explanation, the reveal, the answer, becomes the thing that drives conversion, not the hook itself. The hook's job is to create the information gap. The body's job is to close it with something worth closing. A curiosity hook without a meaningful payoff is clickbait. The close has to earn the open.
Example
"Vets recommend something called occupational chewing therapy to calm dogs naturally." Nobody knows what that means. That's the point.
03
Ingredient
Emotion
Does the hook force a reaction, frustration, recognition, challenge, surprise?
Emotion hooks work by provoking a response before the viewer has consciously decided to engage. They challenge a belief, confirm a frustration, or name something the viewer already knows but hasn't heard said out loud. A neutral hook produces no response and gets scrolled past. An emotional hook, even a mildly uncomfortable one, creates enough activation to hold someone for the next few seconds. Neutral equals scroll.
Example
"Every guy's resolution is to improve their body. Barely any are trying to improve their face, which is quicker and easier." Challenges a belief. Forces a reaction.
The visual half

The best line dies in the wrong frame

A hook is not a line of copy. It's a line of copy inside a visual context. The opening frame registers before the words do. Before the viewer has consciously read anything, they've already processed whether this looks like content or an ad. If it looks like an ad, their guard is up. If it looks like content, they're already watching. The script and the visual brief have to be written together, not sequentially.

01
Organic-looking opening
The opening frame shouldn't read as promotional. No brand colours, no product logo, no polished studio lighting in the first seconds. The visual should look like something from the viewer's existing feed: a creator talking directly to camera, a real space, a candid setup. By the time they recognise it's an ad, they're already engaged. The hook line lands before the guard goes up.
What it looks like: a person at a kitchen counter talking, no visible branding, lighting that feels natural rather than shot.
02
Closeups and expressions
A face reacting to something is involuntary viewing. Humans are wired to read faces. It's one of the few things the brain processes before conscious attention kicks in. A closeup of someone looking surprised, concerned, or visibly reacting to something creates a pull to find out what they're responding to. The visual creates the question. The hook line provides the answer.
What it looks like: tight frame on a face mid-expression, then cut to context. The expression stops the scroll; the line gives it meaning.
03
Immediate context drop
Opening in a situation the viewer recognises puts them inside the ad before they've decided to watch it. A messy bathroom being demoed. A contractor mid-job. Someone on the phone getting a quote. The context makes the hook line credible before it's been said, the viewer is already in the right frame of mind when the words land. Context and copy work together rather than the copy having to do all the work alone.
What it looks like: opening frame places viewer directly in a familiar situation. No brand introduction, no setup, straight into the context.
What changes

The hook-first brief

A brief that treats the hook as a preamble to the real creative work produces a different outcome than one that treats it as the primary creative challenge. The structure below shows the difference in where detail and iteration time goes. Not as a rule for how to write briefs, but as a way of showing what it looks like when the allocation actually matches the importance.

Where the detail goes

How to run this

Hook-first in practice

This isn't a complete overhaul of how you brief creative. It's a reordering of where the thinking happens. These four steps are how the hook-first approach works inside a normal production workflow.

01
Write the hook before you write the script
Start the brief with the hook, not the concept. Who are you speaking to? What are they feeling when they see this ad? What reaction do you want to force in the first three seconds? Write three to five hook variations before touching the script. The concept should emerge from the hook, not the other way around.
02
Brief the visual alongside the copy
Every hook variation should have a visual note, what the opening frame looks like, what the setting is, whether the hook lands on a face or a context shot or a product. Copy and visual are one brief, not two sequential ones. The visual registers first. If it reads as an ad from frame one, the copy has more work to do than it should.
03
Test hooks as statics before scripting
Launch hook variations as static ads, same offer, same CTA, different hook and visual. A week of data tells you which hook qualifies the right buyer before any video is shot. This is the step most production processes skip because it adds time at the front. It saves more time overall by stopping you from scripting and shooting around an opening that doesn't work.
04
Carry the same body through multiple hooks
Once the script is written, structure it so hook variations sit at the top and the body carries through unchanged. This isolates what you're learning: different hooks competing for the same audience, with a consistent body, tells you which opening works without confounding the data. Different hooks within the same concept typically produce one clear winner, find it before you scale, not after.
